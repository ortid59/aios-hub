How to Use This Investment Portal

This guide is written for Richard — a plain-English walkthrough of every section, no financial jargon required. David built this tool so you can explore your options, ask great questions, and feel confident about where your money is going.

1

Build Your Portfolio — Pick Your Funds

This is the most important section. You have 3 portfolio "slots" (A, B, and C) — think of them as three different recipes you can try. Start with Portfolio A. You'll see a list of all the funds available in your Miami DROP plan. Each fund has a percentage input next to it. Type in how much of your money (as a percentage) you want in each fund. For example, if you want half your money in the Vanguard 500 Index, type 50 next to it. All your percentages have to add up to exactly 100. The tool will show you how close you are.

New to this? Start with one of the preset buttons — Conservative, Moderate, or Growth. These are starting points David has suggested. You can always adjust from there.
2

Understand Your Blended Return

Once you pick your funds and set your percentages, the portal automatically calculates your blended return. This is an average of all your funds' historical 10-year performance, weighted by how much you put in each one. A higher number means more growth potential — but also more ups and downs. A lower number is smoother and more stable, but grows slower. There's no "right" answer — it depends on what lets you sleep at night.

Important: These are historical numbers from the past — not a promise of future results. The market could do better or worse. We use them to give you a reasonable baseline for planning.
3

Apply the Portfolio and See Your Projections

Once you're happy with a portfolio allocation, click "Apply to Projections." This updates the Growth Projections section (below) with your exact numbers. You'll see a chart showing how your account could grow over 7 years, and milestone cards showing projected balances at 1, 3, 5, and 7 years — based on your $7,000/month contributions starting from your current $15,355 balance.

Try all three portfolios — build A, B, and C with different approaches, then compare. Seeing the difference between conservative, moderate, and aggressive side-by-side often makes the choice much clearer.
4

Learn About the Funds

Scroll to the "Understanding Your Investments" section. You'll see four boxes — one for each type of fund available in your plan. Click any box to expand it and read a plain-English explanation of what that fund type is, how it behaves in good and bad markets, and when it makes sense to use it. This is your education center.

5

Use the Bear Market Simulator

A bear market is when stock prices fall significantly — sometimes for months or years. This simulator helps you see what would happen to YOUR account if that happened. There are three sliders:

Slider 1 — Market Decline: How far the market drops. Move it left for a mild dip (−10%), right for a severe crash (−60%).

Slider 2 — Duration: How many months the downturn lasts. Anywhere from 6 months to 3 years.

Slider 3 — Timing: When in your 7-year plan the bear market hits. Early? Late? See how it changes the outcome.

The chart shows three lines: (1) No bear market at all, (2) Bear market happens but you keep investing your $7,000/month, (3) Bear market happens and you stop investing out of fear. The key lesson: the green line (keep investing) usually ends up ahead — because you're buying cheap shares while prices are low.

This is the most important part for you, Richard. At 70 with a 7-year horizon, a bear market mid-way through your plan is survivable — especially with dollar cost averaging. This tool proves it with your real numbers.
6

Browse All Available Funds

The Fund Library at the bottom shows every fund in your Miami DROP plan. You can filter by type (Bond, Stock, etc.) and see how each fund has performed over 1, 3, 5, and 10 years. The dots next to each fund show its risk level — 1 dot is very safe, 5 dots is more volatile. Use this as a reference when building your portfolios.

Understanding Your Investments
Click any card to learn more. Plain English — no jargon.
🏦
Stable Value / Cash
● Very Low Risk
Think of this as a savings account inside your retirement plan. Your money doesn't fluctuate. It earns a guaranteed interest rate.

The Massmutual Guaranteed Interest Account is where your money sits right now. It's backed by an insurance contract, so your balance never goes down due to market conditions.

When it makes sense: As a safe "anchor" for a portion of your portfolio — especially to balance out riskier stock funds. Not ideal as your only holding if you want growth over 7+ years.

The trade-off: You won't lose money, but inflation may erode purchasing power over time. A 3% return barely keeps up with average inflation.

In your plan: Massmutual Guaranteed Interest Account (~3.1% historical)
📊
Bond Funds
● Low Risk
When you invest in bonds, you're essentially lending money to the government or big companies. They pay you back with interest over time.

Bonds are much more stable than stocks. They don't typically shoot up or crash dramatically. The Vanguard Total Bond Market holds thousands of different bonds, which spreads the risk.

When it makes sense: As a stabilizing "shock absorber" in your portfolio. When stocks fall, bonds often hold steady or even rise slightly, smoothing out your overall balance.

The trade-off: Low volatility means lower growth. Bond funds have historically returned around 1–3% annually — solid protection but not a growth engine.

Recent note: Bond returns have been negative in some recent years due to interest rate increases. This is normal and tends to reverse over time.

In your plan: Vanguard Total Bond Market Index ADM (~1.8% 10-yr)
⚖️
Balanced / Mixed Funds
● Moderate Risk
These funds do the mixing for you — holding both stocks and bonds in one package. Built-in diversification at a moderate risk level.

A balanced fund is like a "set it and forget it" blend. Vanguard Wellington, for example, has been around since 1929 and holds roughly 65% stocks and 35% bonds. It has a remarkable long-term track record.

When it makes sense: As a core holding for someone who wants growth but doesn't want extreme volatility. Wellington tends to lose less in bear markets than pure stock funds, and still grows well in bull markets.

The trade-off: You won't hit the highs of an all-stock portfolio, but you also won't feel the full lows. For most retirees doing DCA, this is a sweet spot.

In your plan: Vanguard Wellington Admiral, MissionSquare Long-Term Growth, MissionSquare Conservative Growth (~5.8–8.9% 10-yr)
📈
Stock (Equity) Funds
● Moderate–High Risk
You own tiny pieces of hundreds or thousands of companies. Over long periods, stocks have historically outperformed everything else — but they go up and down.

The Vanguard 500 Index tracks the 500 largest U.S. companies (Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, etc.). When those companies do well, your balance grows. When they struggle, it dips.

Large-cap funds (like Vanguard 500, Fidelity Contrafund) invest in big, established companies — more stable than smaller companies.
Small-cap funds (like Vanguard Small-Cap) invest in smaller, faster-growing companies — higher potential but more volatile.
International funds (like Vanguard Total Intl) invest overseas — adds global diversification.

The key insight for you: Because you're contributing $7,000 every month, market dips are actually your friend — you buy more shares at a lower price. See the Bear Market Simulator for proof.

In your plan: Vanguard 500, Fidelity Contrafund K, JPMorgan US Equity, Vanguard Small-Cap, and more (~7.8–13.1% 10-yr)
💡 Why Dollar Cost Averaging Works in Your Favor

No Timing Required

By investing $7,000 every month regardless of market conditions, you automatically buy more shares when prices are low and fewer when prices are high — without having to predict anything.

Bear Markets Become Buying Opportunities

When markets drop, your fixed $7,000 buys more units at a discount. That lowers your average cost and supercharges your recovery. The Bear Market Simulator below shows this with your real numbers.

Discipline Beats Prediction

No investor consistently times the market. DCA enforces discipline — you stay invested through ups and downs, letting compounding work uninterrupted. Time in the market beats timing the market.
